The Basic Rules of Sudoku

At its core, a standard Sudoku puzzle consists of a 9x9 grid. This grid is further divided into nine 3x3 blocks. When you start a game, you will notice that some of the squares are already filled with numbers. These are your starting clues.

Your objective is to fill the remaining empty squares using the numbers 1 through 9. However, there are three absolute, unbreakable rules you must follow:

  • Rule 1: Each horizontal row must contain the numbers 1 through 9 exactly once. No repeats!
  • Rule 2: Each vertical column must contain the numbers 1 through 9 exactly once.
  • Rule 3: Each 3x3 block (or region) must contain the numbers 1 through 9 exactly once.

Sudoku Solving Techniques Step by Step

If you want to learn how to solve sudoku fast, you cannot rely on guessing. Guessing in Sudoku is a trap that will inevitably lead to a broken puzzle. Instead, you must use logic and deduction. Let's break down the best sudoku strategies for beginners.

1. Cross-Hatching (The Easy Way to Solve Sudoku Puzzles)

This is the most fundamental technique. To cross-hatch, pick a specific number (for example, the number 5). Look at a 3x3 block that does not currently contain a 5. Now, look at the neighboring rows and columns to see if a 5 already exists there. Because a number cannot be repeated in a row or column, you can mentally draw "lines" extending from existing 5s to eliminate potential squares in the block you are examining. Often, you will eliminate so many squares that there is only one valid square left for the 5!

2. Counting

If a row, column, or 3x3 block is almost full, simply count the numbers from 1 to 9 to see what is missing. For example, if a row has 1, 2, 3, 5, 6, 7, 8, and 9, the only logical conclusion is that the remaining empty square must be a 4. This is a very satisfying way to clean up the board.

3. Pencil Marking (Notes)

As puzzles get harder, you won't be able to hold all the possibilities in your head. Most digital platforms (including our real-time multiplayer sudoku app) feature a "Notes" mode. If you deduce that a square can only possibly be a 2 or a 7, you can pencil in tiny numbers. Later, when you finally solve where the 7 goes elsewhere in the grid, you can confidently erase the 7 from your notes and declare that square a 2.
